# Timebanking: State of the Field (2025-2026)

## What Has Changed Since the 2017 EasiTime Feasibility Study

### The Big Picture
- Edgar Cahn (founder of timebanking) passed away 23 January 2022
- 34+ countries have timebanks; 500+ in the US, ~300 in the UK
- The movement is **stable but plateaued** in the UK/US, **growing in Asia** (Hong Kong, South Korea)
- **WHO formally recognised** timebanking as a transformative approach for active aging
- COVID-19 spawned 4,000+ mutual aid groups in the UK, but they largely did **not** evolve into timebanks

### Local Context
- **Big Local is ending March 2026** — the programme that supported 150 communities (including Easington Lane & Moorsley) with £1M each since 2012 is closing
- **Hetton New Dawn remains active** but focused on food bank (Loaves & Fishes at ELCAP) and combating loneliness — not timebanking
- **Durham Exchange** still operates as a nearby timebank (DH1)
- **NHS social prescribing** has massively expanded: 3,500+ link workers in England, plans for 6,500

## Technology Platforms in 2026

| Platform | Status | Notes |
|----------|--------|-------|
| **hOurworld Time and Talents** | Active, growing | Free forever. iOS + Android apps. 388 timebanks worldwide. Inter-timebank transfers |
| **Made Open** | Active, growing | Modern platform. Cross-community credit spending. Used across UK, Europe, Asia, US, NZ |
| **Community Weaver** | Effectively dead | Over 100 timebanks migrated to hOurworld. No longer maintained |
| **TimeRepublik** | Active | 100,000+ members in 110+ countries. Uses "TimeCoins" (15-min units). Exploring blockchain |
| **Time Online 2 (TOL2)** | Active | Timebanking UK's bespoke software. Via TBUK membership |

**Key shift since 2017**: Community Weaver (which was assessed in the feasibility study) is now dead. hOurworld and Made Open are the viable options. Mobile apps now exist but remain basic compared to mainstream apps.

**AI-powered matching does not yet exist** in any timebanking platform — a clear gap and opportunity.

## Academic Evidence (2020-2026)

### Strongest Finding: Aging and Active Later Life
- Quasi-experimental studies in Hong Kong show timebanking **increases volunteering** without crowding out other activities
- Effects strongest among **marginalised subgroups** (low education, multiple chronic conditions)
- Timebanking enhances **empowerment and quality of life** through belonging and community connection
- The aging/gerontology evidence base is now the strongest of any timebanking application area

### Implementation Challenges (England, 2020)
- Person-to-person timebanks had **surprisingly small numbers of actual exchanges**
- Some abandoned the exchange model entirely
- Key barriers: risk/safeguarding bureaucracy, paternalistic professional culture, complexity of the mechanism

### The "Equal Value" Paradox (UK, 2022)
- The founding principle (1hr = 1hr regardless of service) creates tension between:
  - Members with **instrumental motivations** (wanting professional services cheaply)
  - Members with **idealistic motivations** (wanting to build community)
- This is the most-studied criticism in the academic literature

## Why Timebanks Succeed and Fail

### Success Factors
1. A **paid coordinator** who is locally known and trusted
2. A **physical drop-in space** (not just digital)
3. **Embedded in a local organisation** (community centre, housing association, health centre)
4. **Regular social events** building group cohesion
5. **Ongoing recruitment** of local businesses and organisations
6. **Diverse membership** (not just the "usual suspects")
7. A **clear funding model** not solely reliant on time-limited grants

### Primary Failure Mode
**Grant dependency** — when funding ends, the timebank closes. No timebank has achieved genuine financial self-sustainability at scale. This was the unresolved challenge in the EasiTime feasibility study.

### Cautionary Tales
- **Gorbals Timebank (Glasgow)**: 3 years of documented success at "re-stitching social fabric", then closed when external funding ended
- **Many UK person-to-person timebanks**: Found to have surprisingly few actual exchanges (2020 research)

## The Missed Connections

### 1. NHS Social Prescribing
- 1.1-1.4 million patients received social prescribing referrals in 2023
- Timebanking is **not explicitly integrated** into NHS social prescribing at a policy level
- But individual timebanks have been referred to by link workers
- **The bridge has not been built** between NASP/NHS England and Timebanking UK
- The NHS 10-Year Health Plan (2026) emphasises community services — a policy window

### 2. Post-COVID Mutual Aid
- 4,000+ mutual aid groups, but **too informal** to adopt timebanking structure
- The formality of timebanking (credits, software, coordinators, DBS checks) was perceived as unnecessary overhead
- A **lighter-weight, app-based model** might have captured the mutual aid energy, but no such product existed

### 3. Platform Cooperativism
- A cooperatively-owned timebanking app (owned by members, not venture capital) would align with the platform cooperativism movement
- The Platform Cooperativism Consortium (New School, NYC) runs a 2026-2027 Fellowship Programme
- **No one has built this yet**

## Policy and Regulatory Landscape (UK)

| Area | Status |
|------|--------|
| **Tax** | Time credits are not taxable (HMRC treats timebanking as volunteering). No explicit guidance |
| **Benefits** | Timebanking classified as "exempt work" — benefits claimants can participate. No explicit guidance |
| **DBS/Safeguarding** | Required for regulated activities (vulnerable adults, children). Adds bureaucratic overhead |
| **Government funding** | No dedicated national funding. Supported indirectly through lottery, local authority, community development grants |
| **Insurance** | Remains an area of ambiguity — liability for service quality/damage during exchanges |

## The Complementary Currency Ecosystem

| System | Relationship to Timebanking |
|--------|---------------------------|
| **LETS** | Mutual credit, variable rates, economic focus. Stagnant since 1990s |
| **Local currencies** (Bristol Pound etc.) | Most have wound down or gone digital-only |
| **Fureai Kippu** (Japan) | Time credits specifically for elder care. Model for aging societies |
| **Blockchain/crypto** | Exploratory only in timebanking. No significant adoption |
| **Mutual aid** | Shares philosophical roots but operates at lower formality |

Timebanking's strength vs other alternative currencies: it's about **social capital**, not trying to be an alternative economy.

## Future Directions

### 1. Aging Societies (Strongest Growth Vector)
- WHO recognition, Hong Kong evidence base, Swiss Zeitvorsorge model
- "Banking time now for care later" — increasingly attractive as populations age
- This is where the evidence is strongest and policy interest most likely

### 2. Social Prescribing Integration
- The most obvious institutional pathway in the UK
- A timebank as a "prescribed" community activity — structured, evidenced, measurable
- Requires someone to bridge NASP/NHS England and Timebanking UK

### 3. Technology Leapfrog
The biggest opportunity — build a modern, mobile-first timebanking app with:
- AI-powered matching (skills to needs)
- Nextdoor-style local community UX
- Cross-community credit portability
- Integration with social prescribing referral pathways
- Lightweight onboarding
- **No one has built this yet**

### 4. Platform Cooperativism
- A cooperatively-owned timebanking platform (member-owned, not VC-funded)
- Aligns with the Platform Cooperativism movement
- Open for someone to pioneer

### 5. Care Economy Integration
- Timebanking as a mechanism for valuing care work that markets systematically undervalue
- Connects to Wellbeing Economy, Doughnut Economics, Degrowth movements
- Cahn's original insight: "The market economy attaches no value at all to the ability to raise children, care for older people, protect the environment"

### 6. Degrowth and Solidarity Economy
- Academic research positions timebanking as a "prefigurative strategy within a wider degrowth movement"
- Reshapes motivations for labour, relocalises networks of solidarity
